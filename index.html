Product

Values

I set the floor, the team sets the ceiling. With strong product sense and attention to detail, a good PM can ensure that their team ships a product that meets users' needs. However, a great PM knows that it takes an informed and motivated team for an idea to realize its full potential. To drive product quality, I believe in clearly defining a simple product process, and inviting others to participate in it.

Discover rigorously, deliver robustly. I've seen teams ship bad ideas well, and ship good ideas poorly. To move fast and keep tech debt low, I like investing in separate expectations and tooling for discovery and delivery. Teams should be able to move seamlessly between qualitatively and quantitatively de-risking iterative prototypes and delivering robust production code using agile practices. Most critically, they should know which of these set of skills they are expected to use when.

Be curious and courageous. In the early stages of an idea, I like to ask "What would prove that I'm wrong?" and then try to get some quick answers to those questions. If an idea doesn't perform as well as I expected, I look forward to the debrief. Being curious about when and why we are wrong is the quickest path to being consistently right. Curiosity also reveals the full extent of the challenges we face, and challenges are an opportunity for growth.

Engineering

Values

Design with compassion. The best engineers keep radical maintainability in mind when choosing tooling, design patterns, and naming conventions. In code reviews I keep an eye out for clear naming, intuitive construction, and, where necessary, clarifying comments. Working code that cannot be easily understood is a liability, period.

Expect collaboration. I believe that defaulting to sharing problems is the fastest path to a happy and sustainably productive team. I embrace pairing, mobbing, distributing credit, and hesitating to assign blame. I seek to create cultures where asking questions is rewarded, at all levels. My best advice for becoming a senior engineer is to act like a junior engineer, until you find yourself on the other side of the questions.

Care about results. Did your code make users' lives better? Did it make the business more secure?
